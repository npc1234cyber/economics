PART 1: ANSWER STRUCTURES BY MARK LEVEL
2 Marks — Calculation
Types: % change, elasticity, index number, average, median.

Rules:

Show ALL working (formula → substitution → answer).
Include correct units, currency symbols, decimal places, and % signs.
Be fast and accurate — no explanation needed.
4 Marks — Explain Using Data
Structure (2 + 2):

2 marks: Explain in detail using a chain of reasoning (cause → effect).
2 marks: Use data from the extract to support/back up your explanation.
Rules:

No evaluation needed.
Data must directly support your reasoning, not just be quoted.
Be concise — time is limited.
9 Marks — Short Structured Response
Structure:

Define 1–2 key terms from the question.
Make a basic analytical point.
Use evidence/quote from the extract.
Draw and explain a diagram (where appropriate).
Chain of analysis referring to the diagram.
Rules:

Do NOT write too much — protect time for 15/25 markers.
You MUST refer to the extract (quote or use data).
Include a diagram where the topic allows it.
No evaluation is required.
Example pattern (negative externality):

Define market failure + negative externality → worded example from extract → diagram (MSC > MPC) → explain overproduction at q vs socially optimal q1 → welfare loss triangle ABE → therefore market failure.

15 Marks — Explain Essay
Structure (choose one):

Option A: 2 definitions + 3 succinct analysis points (recommended).
Option B: 2 definitions + 2 detailed analysis points.
Each analysis point must contain:

Chain of analysis (cause → mechanism → effect).
Diagram, explained and referred to in the text.
Real-world example or data.
Link back to the question.
Rules:

No evaluation required (AQA). Check your board.
Start with 1–2 definitions.
Aim for at least 1 diagram, ideally 2.
Include 3 pieces of real-world application across the answer.
25 Marks — Evaluate Essay
Structure:

Introduction:   Define 2 key terms. State your argument briefly. Get into body fast.
Analysis 1:     Chain of reasoning + diagram + real-world example.
Evaluation 1:   "This depends on..." + explain + pick a side/judgement + link to question.
Analysis 2:     Chain of reasoning + diagram + real-world example.
Evaluation 2:   "This depends on..." + explain + pick a side/judgement + link to question.
Analysis 3:     Chain of reasoning + (optional diagram) + real-world example.
Evaluation 3:   "This depends on..." + explain + pick a side/judgement + link to question.
Conclusion:     Answer the question + justify + introduce one new evaluation point.
Alternative: 2 long A+E pairs instead of 3, with an extended conclusion.

Analysis paragraph checklist:

 Clear chain of reasoning (every logical step stated).
 Diagram with SCALE (Shift, Coordinates, Axes, Labels, Explanation in text).
 Real-world example or extract data.
 Link back to the question.
Evaluation paragraph checklist:

 State: "This depends on [factor]."
 Explain WHY it depends on this factor.
 Pick a side or extend reasoning (use theory/evidence).
 Link back to the question — how does this change the answer?
Conclusion checklist:

 Directly answer the question.
 Justify with reference to your strongest point(s).
 Introduce one NEW evaluative factor the answer depends on.
PART 2: EVALUATION FRAMEWORK
How to Evaluate (universal template)
State: "This depends on [factor]."
Explain: Why the previous analysis depends on this factor.
Judge: Pick a side using theory or real-world evidence. OR extend reasoning further.
Link: How does this evaluation change or support the answer to the question?
20 Key Evaluation Points

#	Factor	When to use	Example application
1	Price elasticity of demand	Tax, subsidy, max/min price essays	Sugar tax: addictive goods → inelastic PED → small quantity fall → tax ineffective at improving health
2	Business objectives	Market structure essays	Monopoly with satisficing objective may set lower price → less welfare loss
3	Size of firm vs MES	After discussing economies of scale	If firm exceeds MES → diseconomies of scale → bureaucracy → higher LRAC

7	How firm allocates profit	After dynamic efficiency argument	Profits may go to dividends not investment (Thames Water example) → no cost reduction or quality improvement
8	Price cap level / regulatory capture	Competition policy, regulation essays	Cap too high = ineffective; regulators may be "captured" by firms
9	Time frame (SR vs LR)	Elasticity, market structure essays	PED may become more elastic in LR as substitutes emerge → tax more effective over time

#	Factor	When to use	Example application
10	Spare capacity	Any AD shift discussion	Low spare capacity (UK unemployment ~4%) → AD shift right → mainly inflation, little GDP growth (Keynesian AS)
11	Level of interest rates	Fiscal policy, government borrowing	Bank Rate at 4.25% (May 2025) → high borrowing costs → larger opportunity cost of fiscal expansion
12	Position on Laffer curve	Tax change essays	If already past revenue-maximising point → higher tax rate reduces revenue

19	Level of confidence	Tax cuts, interest rate changes	Low consumer confidence → tax cuts saved not spent → smaller C increase → smaller AD shift

PART 3: KEY DIAGRAMS
How to Draw Diagrams — SCALE
Letter	Meaning
S	Show the Shift of curve(s)
C	Label Coordinates of key points (p, p1, q, q1)
A	Label Axes (Price/Cost, Quantity/Real GDP)
L	Label all curves (S, S1, D, AD, LRAS, etc.)
E	Explain the diagram in the text
Essential Micro Diagrams
Supply and demand (shifts, consumer/producer surplus, price mechanism)
Tax and subsidy (with incidence shading)
Maximum / minimum price
Negative externality (MSC > MPC, or MSB < MPB; welfare loss triangle)
Positive externality (MSB > MPB, or MSC < MPC)
Monopoly cost-revenue (MC=MR for profit max, supernormal profit area)
Other objectives on cost-revenue (sales max: AR=ATC; revenue max: MR=0)
LRAC curve (economies/diseconomies of scale, MES)
Price discrimination (whole market + elastic/inelastic submarkets)
Labour market supply and demand (wage determination, discrimination)
Tradable pollution permits (supply shift left → price rise)
Contestable markets (AR=ATC due to threat of entry)
High-Level Micro Diagram Techniques
Show price mechanism (arrows showing adjustment to equilibrium).
Label consumer surplus / producer surplus changes.
Layer tax/subsidy onto externality diagram.
Show shifts in MC/ATC on cost-revenue diagrams (dynamic efficiency).
Show supernormal profit area and how it changes.
Essential Macro Diagrams
AD-AS (classical and Keynesian LRAS)
AD shift right with multiplier effect (AD → AD1 → AD2)
SRAS shift left (cost-push inflation)
LRAS shift right (supply-side policies)
Combined AD + SRAS shifts (e.g. depreciation: AD right, SRAS left)
Laffer curve
Tariff diagram (with welfare loss, govt revenue, CS/PS changes)
Currency supply and demand
Phillips curve (short-run and expectations-augmented long-run)
High-Level Macro Diagram Techniques
Double AD shifts for multiplier effect.
Simultaneous SRAS + LRAS shifts from same cause.
Keynesian LRAS to show spare capacity argument.
Movement along the curve not shifting (extension/contraction in AD or AS).
Tariff diagrams with welfare areas labelled.

PART 5: ANALYSIS TECHNIQUES
Microeconomics Analysis — Extending Points
For consumers: Price effect → consumer surplus change → quality effects.

For firms: Use "PIES" — Profits, Investment, Employment/Efficiency, Shutdown risk.

For externalities: Welfare loss/gain area → potential policy revenue uses.

For labour markets: Worker surplus → poverty/inequality effects.

Further firm responses: A sugar tax → firms switch to non-sugary drinks. A subsidy → firms may pocket it as dividends.

Macroeconomics Analysis — Extending Points
For AD shifts: Always consider the multiplier effect (AD shifts twice: AD → AD1 → AD2).

For AS shifts: All 4 macro objectives usually move together (LRAS right = growth up, inflation down, unemployment down, exports more competitive).

Second-round effects: AD shift → employment change → consumption change → further AD shift.

Link to macro objectives: After any shift, state effects on growth, inflation, unemployment, and current account.

Key Macro Equations
AD = C + I + G + (X - M)
Multiplier = 1 / (1 - MPC) or 1 / (MPT + MPS + MPM)
Marshall-Lerner Condition: |PED_X| + |PED_M| > 1 → depreciation improves net exports

PART 8: TOPIC-SPECIFIC ESSAY PATTERNS
Externality / Government Intervention Essay
A1: Tax/subsidy/regulation → diagram → reduces/increases quantity → welfare improvement.
E1: PED may be inelastic → small quantity change → limited effectiveness.
A2: Tradable permits / alternative policy → diagram → mechanism.
E2: Government may lack information → set wrong level → government failure.
A3: Subsidy for substitutes / information provision → mechanism.
E3: Firms may pocket subsidy / consumers may not respond to information.
Conclusion: Best policy + depends on [elasticity / government information / market structure].
Market Structure Essay
A1: Profit max (MC=MR) → supernormal profit → dynamic efficiency → R&D → lower costs / better quality.
E1: Profits may go to dividends not investment (Thames Water / Apple 25% dividend ratio).
A2: Alternative objective (sales max / revenue max) → economies of scale → lower LRAC.
E2: Firm may exceed MES → diseconomies of scale → bureaucracy.
Conclusion: Profit max best in LR; may pursue other objectives in SR. Depends on competition policy.
Macroeconomic Policy Essay
A1: AD effect → multiplier → growth + inflation tradeoff. Diagram: AD shifts right twice.
E1: Spare capacity / proportion of AD affected / confidence level.
A2: Supply-side effect → LRAS shift → all objectives improve. Diagram: LRAS right.
E2: Time lags / inequality from market-based policies / budget deficit from interventionist policies.
Conclusion: SR vs LR effects. Depends on current economic conditions.
Currency / Trade Essay
A1: Depreciation → exports cheaper, imports dearer → net exports rise → AD right → growth.
E1: Marshall-Lerner condition may not hold (PEDs too inelastic in SR).
A2: Higher import costs → SRAS left → cost-push inflation → reduces purchasing power.
E2: Many firms don't rely on imports (labour-intensive services).
Conclusion: Mainly inflationary. SR vs LR (J-curve: PEDs become more elastic over time).
